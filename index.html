Hot peppers

Hot peppers

Why are they hot?

Capsaicin is a natural alkaloid made from hot peppers. This alkaloid is to blame for the pepper's characteristic angry taste. Different types of peppers and pepperoni contain different amounts of capsaicin.
Capsaicin or capsacin is actually a very complex material. It does not dissolve in water, but it dissolves readily in alcohol and oils. When applied to the skin, it causes burning sensation and hyperemia (increased circulation in the treated area), which helps to relieve rheumatic pain.

​Carolina Reaper

The hottest peppers on the world

​Carolina Reaper is a cultivar of the Capsicum chinense plant. Developed by South Carolina grower Ed Currie, the pepper is red and purple with a furrowed texture and a small pointed tail.  In 2013, the Guinness World Record declared him the world's hottest chili pepper, surpassing the former record holder, the Trinidadian Scorpion "Butch T."  reaper.

history of peppers

Spicy pepper in Native American tradition

Capsicum fruits were part of the human diet of about 7,500 p. n. They are one of the oldest cultivated crops in the Americas. Chilli cultivation originates in northeastern Mexico some 6,000 years ago. Chili was one of the first self-pollinated crops cultivated in Mexico, Central America, and parts of South America.

Traces of capsaicin on pottery

Pottery that had a positive test for traces of Capsicum sp. of the remains, and which was excavated at Chiapa de Korzo in southern Mexico, dates from the Middle to Late Classical period (400 BC to 300 AD).
